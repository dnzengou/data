PROBLEMS WORTH SOLVING

1Ô∏è‚É£ Problem sources


By far the most common mistake startups make is to build solutions to problems that don't really exist, or that has a very niched target group. This is why industry experience within the co-founder team is so important - if you've felt the pains yourself while working in the industry, chances are others have too and that they're willing to pay for a solution. 

Consider these questions to ponder:
  • Are there obvious opportunities in the industries you have worked in? 
  • Can you think of any broken processes, outdated technologies, unserved customers, fragmented competitors?
  • Are there any business models that have not been applied to a field of your expertise?
The right solution will come by consciously defining and framing the problem - truly understanding the problem before creating the solution.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Look for problems that are worth solving


To gain a true competitive advantage, you have to recognise and fully understand the core problem of your customers. Only then, can you develop a great solution. However, before you start considering solutions to a problem, make sure the problem is worth solving. Here are some key characteristics you should look for üëá 

ü§¨ Painful. Look for problems that are so painful that users are willing to pay significant amounts of their spending power for potential solutions.

ü§© Popular. How many are experiencing this problem? Will your solution be used by millions of users?

üîÅ Frequent. How often are users experiencing this problem? Look for regular, recurring, frequent problems that users run into in their daily life.

‚ùóÔ∏è Urgent. Seek for problems that urgently need to be solved right now. It might for instance be new regulations coming in place that presents opportunities.

üìà Growing. How does the market look? Is it seeing significant growth? Typically, we want to see growth of around +20% per year.

Yet, a problem worth solving alone is certainly no guarantee for a successful innovation project or business model. Too many other factors, such as the timing of a solution or the composition of the team, also play a large role. However, without a problem worth solving, failure is almost guaranteed.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Dig deeper


Naturally, a common question that comes up is how you can dig deeper in your problem and validate whether you're onto something worth solving. To help steer you in the right direction, here‚Äôs a useful process for truly understanding the problem before you try to solve it.

It's called Root Cause Analysis üëá

Step 1Ô∏è‚É£: Define the Problem
  • What do you see happening?
  • What are the specific symptoms?
Step 2Ô∏è‚É£: Collect Data
  • What proof do you have that the problem exists?
  • How long has the problem existed?
  • What is the impact of the problem?
A helpful tool at this stage is CATWOE. With this process, you look at the same situation from different perspectives: the Customers, the people (Actors) who implement the solutions, the Transformation process that‚Äôs affected, the World view, the process Owner, and Environmental constraints.

Step 3Ô∏è‚É£: Identify Possible Causal Factors
  • What sequence of events leads to the problem?
  • What conditions allow the problem to occur?
  • What other problems surround the occurrence of the central problem?
During this stage, identify as many causal factors as possible. Too often, people identify one or two factors and then stop, but that‚Äôs not sufficient. With RCA, you don‚Äôt want to simply treat the most obvious causes ‚Äì you want to dig deeper ü§ø

Use these tools to help identify causal factors:
  • Appreciation ‚Äì Use the facts and ask ‚ÄúSo what?‚Äù to determine all the possible consequences of a fact.
  • 5 Whys ‚Äì Ask ‚ÄúWhy?‚Äù until you get to the root of the problem.
  • Drill Down ‚Äì Break down a problem into small, detailed parts to better understand the big picture.
  • Cause and Effect Diagrams ‚Äì Create a chart of all of the possible causal factors, to see where the trouble may have begun.
Step 4Ô∏è‚É£: Identify the Root Cause(s)
  • Why does the causal factor exist?
  • What is the real reason the problem occurred?
Use the same tools you used to identify the causal factors (in Step Three) to look at the roots of each factor. These tools are designed to encourage you to dig deeper at each level of cause and effect.

Step 5Ô∏è‚É£: Recommend and Implement Solutions
  • What can you do to prevent the problem from happening again?
  • How will the solution be implemented?
  • Who will be responsible for it?
  • What are the risks of implementing the solution?
One way of doing this is to use Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA). This tool builds on the idea of risk analysis to identify points where a solution could fail. Impact Analysis is another useful tool here. This helps you explore possible positive and negative consequences of a change on different parts of a system or organisation. Another great strategy to adopt is Kaizen, or continuous improvement. This is the idea that continual small changes create better systems overall.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Keep up with industry trends


We are often tempted to jump right into the perfect idea, but taking the time to develop a good understanding of ongoing trends is very valuable.

Consider these questions to ponder:
  • What are the major technology trends both inside and outside your market of interest?
  • Which technologies represent important opportunities or disruptive threats
  • Which regulatory trends influence your market of interest?
  • How would you characterize income and wealth distribution in your market
  • Which trends might influence buyer behavior?
